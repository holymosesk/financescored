Student loans explained: what changed in 2026, and how to not get burned

What is a student loan, really?

A student loan is money borrowed to pay for education, repaid later with interest. That part's simple. The complexity is in the type of loan you have, because different types behave in very different ways.

Loan TypeWho It's ForInterest Starts
Direct SubsidizedUndergrads with financial needAfter you leave school, following a 6-month grace period. The government pays interest while you're in school.
Direct UnsubsidizedUndergrad & grad students, no need requiredImmediately, the day the loan is disbursed — including while you're still in school.
Parent PLUSParents of dependent undergradsImmediately. Capped at $20,000/year and $65,000 total per student as of the 2026-27 school year.
Grad PLUSGraduate studentsEliminated for new borrowers as of July 2026.
PrivateAnyone, via a bank or private lenderVaries by lender. No federal income-driven repayment or forgiveness eligibility, ever.

Private loans are the least flexible item on this list. Whatever the lender's contract says, goes — no income-driven repayment, no federal forgiveness path, no guaranteed deferment. If part of your debt is private, don't assume federal-loan rules apply to it.

The part that quietly costs people money

If you don't pay the interest accruing on an unsubsidized or PLUS loan, most servicers capitalize it — meaning the unpaid interest gets added to your principal balance, and you then pay interest on that interest too.

A $10,000 unsubsidized loan sitting through four years of school plus a six-month grace period, accruing at 6%, can easily grow to $12,500+ before your first payment is even due. You didn't borrow more. The loan just grew while nobody was looking.

What changed in 2026 (and why it actually matters to you)

In July 2025, Congress passed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). Most of its student loan provisions took effect July 1, 2026. Here's what changed, without the spin:

The old income-driven repayment plans are being phased out

SAVE, PAYE, and Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR) stopped accepting new enrollments as of July 1, 2026, and will be fully gone by July 1, 2028.

Old SystemNew System (loans disbursed after 7/1/26)
SAVE, PAYE, ICR, IBRRAP (Repayment Assistance Plan) — new income-driven plan
Standard 10-year planNew Standard Plan — fixed payments over 10, 15, 20, or 25 years based on balance

Public Service Loan Forgiveness and IDR forgiveness are still intact

No new forgiveness program was created, but none of the existing ones were eliminated either. PSLF is unchanged. IDR forgiveness after 20–30 years of payments is still on the books.

Mark this date: June 30, 2028

If your loans were disbursed before July 1, 2026, you get a choice: stay on your current plan, switch to Income-Based Repayment (IBR, which wasn't eliminated), or opt into RAP. But you have to actively choose by June 30, 2028. Miss it, and you're automatically enrolled in RAP whether you looked into it or not. RAP might genuinely be right for you — but "might be right" and "didn't choose" shouldn't be the same outcome.

One more thing worth budgeting for: forgiveness discharged after December 31, 2025 is taxable income again at the federal level. A forgiven balance can show up as a tax bill the following spring.

Where people get burned

  • Not knowing which loans are accruing interest right now. Log into studentaid.gov and actually look. Unsubsidized loans grow quietly while you're in school, whether you think about them or not.
  • Choosing a plan based on the lowest monthly payment alone. A longer term or income-driven plan can lower your payment while dramatically increasing total interest paid over the life of the loan.
  • Missing the 2028 deadline by accident. Doing nothing has a default outcome now — automatic enrollment in RAP.
  • Not budgeting for a forgiveness tax bill. If you're on a 20+ year forgiveness track, the eventual forgiven amount may be taxable. Worth knowing now, not the day it arrives.
  • Treating private loans like federal loans. No income-driven repayment, no federal forgiveness path, no guaranteed deferment options.

What to actually do

  1. Log into studentaid.gov and get a clear list of every federal loan you have — type, balance, and current interest rate.
  2. Figure out your interest-accrual status. Subsidized and paused, or unsubsidized and accruing right now?
  3. If your loans predate July 2026, put the June 30, 2028 decision deadline in your phone today — not as a someday, as an actual date.
  4. Run the numbers on total cost, not just monthly payment, before choosing a repayment plan.
  5. If forgiveness is part of your plan, start setting aside a small amount now for a possible future tax bill. Nobody regrets being early on that one.

Frequently asked questions

When does interest start on student loans?

Subsidized federal loans don't accrue interest while you're in school or during your grace period. Unsubsidized and PLUS loans start accruing interest the day the money is disbursed, even while you're still in school.

What is the Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)?

RAP is the new income-driven repayment plan created by the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act, available for loans disbursed after July 1, 2026, and as an option for some earlier borrowers.

Is my old repayment plan going away?

SAVE, PAYE, and ICR stopped accepting new enrollments on July 1, 2026, and fully end by July 1, 2028. If you're already enrolled, you can stay until then, but you'll need to choose IBR or RAP before the deadline.

Is student loan forgiveness taxable?

Forgiveness discharged after December 31, 2025 is taxable as federal income. PSLF and IDR forgiveness programs themselves are still intact; it's the tax treatment of the forgiven amount that changed.

What happens if I do nothing before the 2028 deadline?

If your loans predate July 2026 and you don't actively choose a repayment plan by June 30, 2028, you'll be automatically enrolled in the Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP).
