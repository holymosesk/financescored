$75,000 salary: how much house can you afford?

Short answer: somewhere between $285,000 and $505,000. The real answer depends entirely on your other debt — here's the math for all three tiers.

Your Range on $75,000

$285,000 – $505,000

Assumes 6.5% 30-yr fixed rate, 20% down. Don't have 20%? See the down payment breakdown below — it changes less than you'd think.

Here's the thing about "how much house can I afford" calculators: most of them show you the cautious textbook number and stop there, which is a little like a doctor only ever giving you the worst-case diagnosis. On $75,000, the honest answer is a range — and where you actually land in that range comes down to your other debt more than almost anything else.

What that actually looks like month to month

Take the Typical-tier home at $445,000. Put 20% down ($89,000), borrow $356,000, and at 6.5% your principal and interest lands around $2,249/month. Add roughly $501 for taxes and insurance and you're at about $2,750 total — right at that 44% back-end line.

Drop to the Conservative tier at $285,000 and your all-in payment falls to about $1,750/month — noticeably more breathing room, but also a smaller home for the same income. Neither number is "correct." They're two honest answers to two different questions: what can you qualify for, versus what leaves you room to breathe.

PMI vs. FHA MIP, in plain English

PMI shows up on conventional loans when you put down less than 20%. It's not a scam — it's what lets the lender approve a smaller down payment at all — and it goes away automatically once you hit 20% equity. Cost depends heavily on credit score, roughly 0.5% to 1.5% of the loan per year.

FHA MIP works differently: every FHA loan carries it, no matter the down payment, and it usually sticks around for the life of the loan unless you refinance into a conventional loan later. It has an upfront piece (1.75% of the loan, usually rolled in) plus a smaller annual piece (around 0.5-0.55%).

Bottom line: if you're choosing between 5% conventional and 3.5% FHA, the deciding factor usually isn't the home price — they land close to each other. It's your credit score (FHA is more forgiving), how much cash you have today, and whether you plan to refinance out of MIP down the road.

Mistakes that quietly cost $75K earners the most

  • Not accounting for other debt in the Typical/Aggressive numbers. The $445,000 and $505,000 figures assume minimal other monthly debt. A $500/month car payment pulls the Typical number down to roughly $365,000 — an $80,000 swing from one payment.
  • Forgetting HOA fees aren't in the math above. If the home has an HOA, that fee eats directly into your housing budget dollar-for-dollar — a $150/month HOA functions the same as a higher mortgage payment.
  • Treating pre-approval as a done deal. Financing a car or opening a new credit card between pre-approval and closing can knock you out of your tier entirely. Lenders re-check before closing, not just at the start.
  • Ignoring how much a credit score swing changes this. A 620 vs. a 760 score can move your rate by half a point or more — worth tens of thousands in buying power on the exact same $75,000 salary.

Frequently asked questions

Can I afford a $400,000 house on a $75,000 salary?

Yes, if your other debt is low. $400,000 sits comfortably within the Typical tier (~$445,000) and well within the Aggressive/FHA tier (~$505,000). It's a stretch at the Conservative tier ($285,000) but realistic in real-world underwriting.

How much is the monthly payment on a $445,000 house?

With 20% down at 6.5%, expect roughly $2,249/month in principal and interest, plus about $501/month for taxes and insurance — about $2,750/month total.

What if I have other debt, like a car payment?

It comes directly out of your housing budget. A $500/month car payment can reduce your realistic price range by roughly $80,000, since it counts against the same back-end DTI ratio your mortgage does.

Do I really need 20% down to buy a house?

No. On a $75,000 salary at the Typical tier, 5% down (conventional, with PMI) or 3.5% down (FHA, with MIP) both land around $353,000-$356,000 — barely less than the $445,000 you'd get with 20% down — while needing $70,000+ less cash upfront.
