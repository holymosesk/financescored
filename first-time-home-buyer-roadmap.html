First-time home buyer roadmap: every step, in order

Ten stages, realistic timeframes, and what actually happens at each one — so nothing on closing day is a surprise.

Most people learn the home buying process backwards — by making a mistake at step 6 that a decision at step 1 would have prevented. Here's the order that actually works, with links to the specifics at each stage.

The 10 steps

1
Build your credit
Months to years before
Your credit score drives your interest rate more than almost anything else. Start here if your score isn't where it needs to be — everything downstream is easier with a stronger score.
2
Save for your down payment
Months before
You don't need 20% — 5% conventional and 3.5% FHA are both real, common paths. Know your target before you start looking at homes, not after.
See down payment scenarios →
3
Get pre-approved
Before you shop, not after
Pre-approval is what tells you your real number, and it's what sellers actually trust. Decide which lender type fits your situation and gather your documents before you apply.
Bank vs. broker vs. lender →  ·  Application checklist →
4
Find a realtor
1-2 weeks
A buyer's agent is typically paid by the seller's side of the transaction, so there's rarely a reason to shop without one. Look for someone who knows your target area, not just anyone with a license.
5
Shop for homes
2-8 weeks
Shop inside your pre-approved range, not your dream range. This is where the Conservative-vs-Typical DTI math from your affordability number actually matters in real life.
6
Make an offer
1-3 days
Your agent will help structure price, contingencies, and earnest money. In competitive markets, a clean offer with fewer contingencies often beats a slightly higher one with more strings attached.
7
Complete inspections
7-10 days after acceptance
This is your chance to find out what you're actually buying before you're financially committed. Don't skip it to seem more competitive — it's the one step that protects you, not the seller.
8
Appraisal
1-2 weeks after inspection
Your lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm the home is worth what you're paying. If it comes in low, you, the seller, or both will need to work out the gap.
9
Final loan approval
2-4 weeks
Underwriting does a final review of your full file. This is exactly why you don't finance a car or open new credit during this window — see the checklist's "don't do these" section for the full list.
Mistakes that delay closing →
10
Closing day
The finish line
You'll sign a stack of paperwork, pay your down payment and closing costs, and get your keys. Bring a valid photo ID and be ready for the closing disclosure numbers to match what you were expecting — that's what the earlier steps were for.

Realistic timeline, start to finish

For someone with credit and savings already in decent shape: roughly 2-4 months from starting your home search to getting keys. Once you're under contract, closing itself typically takes 30-45 days — inspection, appraisal, and final underwriting all happen in that window.

If you're starting from steps 1 or 2 — building credit or saving a down payment — add however long that honestly takes. There's no shortcut worth rushing, and a few extra months of preparation almost always beats a rejected loan application.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to buy a house from start to finish?

For someone with credit and savings already in decent shape, roughly 2-4 months from starting your home search to closing. If you're building credit or saving a down payment first, add however long that honestly takes.

What's the very first step in buying a house?

Check your credit and get pre-approved before you start looking at homes, not after. Shopping for a house before you know your real number is how people fall in love with something they can't actually afford.

How long does closing actually take once I'm under contract?

Typically 30-45 days from an accepted offer to closing day, covering inspection, appraisal, and final loan underwriting.
