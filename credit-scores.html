Why this section exists

A credit score gets treated like a mystery — something that moves for reasons nobody quite explains. It isn't. It's a weighted formula built from five public, well-documented factors, and understanding the weighting tells you exactly where to focus instead of guessing.

This section starts with the full breakdown, then goes deep on the one factor that moves fastest and surprises people the most: credit utilization.

The two factors that matter most

Payment history (35%) is the foundation — a long record of paying on time, built slowly over years. Credit utilization (30%) is the lever that moves fastest, recalculated every billing cycle rather than built up gradually. Together, these two factors make up roughly two-thirds of a FICO score. If you only manage two things well, these are the two.

Mistakes that quietly cost people the most

  • Closing old cards to "clean up" credit. This can shrink both your average account age and your total available credit — two factors, not one, working against you at the same time.
  • Assuming a low score is permanent. It reflects past behavior, not a life sentence. Consistent on-time payments and controlled utilization can rebuild a score meaningfully over time.
  • Ignoring utilization until right before a big application. Because it's recalculated every cycle, it responds faster than any other factor — a real advantage if you plan ahead of a mortgage or auto loan application instead of scrambling the week before.
  • Treating a good score as a reason to apply for everything you qualify for. Good credit should create options, not temptation to open accounts you don't actually need.

Frequently asked questions

What's the fastest way to improve a credit score?

Paying every bill on time is the single biggest factor and the foundation everything else builds on. After that, lowering credit utilization is the fastest-moving lever, since it's recalculated every billing cycle rather than built up slowly over years like payment history and account age.

Can a low credit score really be fixed?

Yes. A low score reflects past behavior, not a permanent state — consistent on-time payments and controlled utilization can meaningfully rebuild a score over time. There's no shortcut around the timeline, but the trajectory is very much within your control.

Does my credit score affect more than just loan approval?

Yes. Beyond approval odds, it directly affects the interest rate you're offered on mortgages, auto loans, and credit cards — often the difference between tens of thousands of dollars in lifetime interest on the same loan amount. It can also affect insurance rates, apartment rental applications, and utility deposit requirements in many states.
